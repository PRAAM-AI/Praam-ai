Solution 1

RFP & capture

Grounded proposal & RFP workspace

AI-assisted answers tied to your library — not the open web

Praam helps teams draft proposals and RFP responses from the documents you already trust: past wins, security packs, SOPs, and product sheets. Retrieval stays scoped, generation stays citeable, and reviewers can verify every paragraph against source chunks before anything ships.

From inbox RFQs to award-ready quotes without losing auditability

PRISM turns fragmented email and spreadsheets into one governed thread — structured lines, comparable vendor quotes, and a clear path to ERP prep.

Aviation MRO / parts sourcing (composite) · ~22 procurement & SCE staff

Context

Requirements arrived as unstructured email and PDFs; teams tracked outreach in shared inboxes and side spreadsheets. ERP prep meant manual copy-paste and inconsistent fields — painful in a sector where traceability and approvals matter.

Problem
  • Unstructured requirement email — parts, alternates, and dates buried in threads.
  • Slow vendor outreach, follow-up, and no single place for RFQ status.
  • Hard to compare quotes consistently across vendors and revisions.
  • Compliance pressure: who approved what went out, and which quote line justified the award?
What we built

Intake and extraction with validation, approved-supplier shortlists, RFQ compose and tracking, parsing of vendor replies, comparison views, approval gates, and export / ERP-oriented handoff — AI assists drafting and parsing; rules and humans own decisions. We do not position this as full ERP replacement in production.

How it runs

Email in → structured requirement → shortlist → RFQ → responses → review and approve → downstream export. Assistive models speed drafting and parsing; policy engines and named approvers control go/no-go.

Results

Directional outcomes: fewer missed fields on first pass, faster first RFQ to vendors, fewer manual copy-paste steps into ERP prep packages, and an audit trail reviewers could actually replay — quantitative benchmarks available under NDA for serious evaluations.

What we’d do differently next

Earlier workspace model for multi-RFQ programs and tighter coupling to the customer’s preferred ERP upload templates from day one — lessons that now shape our implementation playbook.

One place for “what does our approved policy actually say?”

From Slack guesses to cited answers — Knowledge Studio grounds responses in the policies and runbooks your risk team already trusts, with a path from chat → edit → export.

Global ops & GRC (composite) · ~40 L1/L2 support and policy analysts

Context

Answers lived in Confluence, PDFs in SharePoint, and “tribal knowledge” in long Slack threads. Generic chat tools were either blocked or hallucinated — neither was acceptable for customer-facing or regulator-touching topics.

Problem
  • High variance in how analysts phrased the same policy answer — hard to train new hires.
  • No fast way to prove which document version backed a decision after the fact.
  • Low trust in “AI summaries” without visible citations to specific paragraphs or tables.
  • Security needed strict collection scope — not whole-tenant search, and not the open web.
Constraints

SSO and group-based access to curated collections; retention and logging aligned with enterprise GRC; answers had to show source passages, not only a model confidence score; deployment in the customer’s preferred cloud region.

What we built

Knowledge Studio: governed ingest of policies, SOPs, and FAQs; natural-language Q&A with retrieval scoped per role; an editor workspace for refinement; citations end-to-end; and hooks to trigger downstream automation once a human marks an answer as approved for reuse.

Results

Directionally: shorter mean time to a defensible answer, fewer escalations to SMEs for repeat questions, and audit conversations that started with “here is the cited chunk” instead of thread archaeology — quantitative metrics available under NDA.

What we’d do differently next

Earlier bulk import of archived ticket macros into the same corpus so suggested replies and policy answers drew from one governed index from week one.
